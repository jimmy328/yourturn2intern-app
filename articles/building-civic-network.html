Networking

Building Your Civic Network Before You Graduate

By Jeremy Fang

If you've never had an internship before, the word "networking" can sound like something other people do, people with connected parents, or a cousin who works in Albany. Here's the truth: almost nobody starts with a network. They build one, slowly, usually starting in high school or their first year of college. You can start this week.

In civic and political work especially, relationships matter as much as resumes. Government offices, campaigns, and nonprofits are small worlds. The person who hires you for a summer internship might also be the person who recommends you for a job three years later, or introduces you to a state senator's chief of staff. None of that happens if nobody knows your name.

Start with people you already see

Your network doesn't begin with VIPs. It begins with your government teacher, your guidance counselor, a professor who mentioned a local election in class. Tell them directly: "I'm interested in finding an internship in local government or with a campaign. Do you know anyone I could talk to?" Most adults are happy to make an introduction when you ask clearly and politely.

Show up where local government happens

Town board meetings, school board meetings, and county legislature sessions are open to the public and usually short on young faces. Attending one, even just once, puts you in the same room as elected officials and their staff. Introduce yourself afterward and say you're a student interested in interning. People remember the student who showed up.

Use LinkedIn like a notebook, not a billboard

You don't need a polished profile to start using it. Search for staff at your town hall or a campaign you admire. Send a short, specific message: mention something concrete, like a program they run or a vote they took, and ask if they'd answer a few questions about their work. Most people say yes to a respectful, brief request.

The power of weak ties

Researchers who study how people find jobs have a name for this: "weak ties." Your close friends and family often know the same things you do. But the acquaintance you met once at a town meeting often has access to a completely different set of opportunities. Don't underestimate a five-minute conversation. It can be the link to an internship months later.

Follow up, since this is the step people skip

After any conversation, send a short thank-you email within a day or two, mentioning one specific thing you talked about. Then, every so often, send a brief update: a new class, an event you attended, a question about their work. You're not asking for anything. You're just staying visible. When an opportunity opens up, you want to be the student they think of first.

You don't need to know the right people. You need to become someone worth knowing, and that starts with showing up.
