Data and Democracy: How Interns Use Analytics on Campaigns

By Jeremy Fang

When people picture a political campaign, they usually imagine yard signs, door-knocking, and speeches. What they don't picture is a spreadsheet. But behind almost every modern campaign, local, county, or statewide, there's a quiet engine of data work, and a lot of it gets done by interns.

Why campaigns rely on data

A campaign is trying to answer a few simple questions: who is likely to vote, who is likely to vote for our candidate, and how do we reach those people efficiently? Answering those questions well requires keeping track of a lot of information, like who's been contacted, what they said, which neighborhoods have been canvassed, and which voters still need a reminder to get to the polls.

Campaigns generally track this through what's often called a "voter file," a working list of registered voters in the area, updated as the campaign learns more about who supports them, who's undecided, and who needs more outreach. Combined with results from phone calls and door-knocking, this builds a general picture of where the campaign should focus its time and volunteers as Election Day approaches.

What an intern's data work actually looks like

If you intern on a campaign, you probably won't be building predictive models. You're more likely to be doing the hands-on work that makes the bigger picture possible:

  • Entering canvassing results after volunteers return from knocking on doors, so the campaign knows who was reached and what they said.
  • Organizing spreadsheets of volunteer sign-ups, event attendance, or donor information.
  • Helping track turnout data on Election Day, so the campaign can identify supporters who haven't voted yet.
  • Cleaning up contact lists by fixing typos, removing duplicates, and making sure addresses are accurate.

It can feel unglamorous compared to door-knocking or being on camera, but this work is exactly what keeps a campaign organized enough to function. Campaigns run on accurate information, and interns are often the ones keeping that information accurate.

Why these skills matter beyond campaigns

The habits you build doing campaign data work, like attention to detail, comfort with spreadsheets, and organizing messy information into something usable, transfer directly to almost any career. Nonprofits, government agencies, and private companies all rely on people who can manage data carefully and spot when something doesn't add up. A summer of campaign data work is a legitimate skill-builder, even if it doesn't feel that way while you're doing it.

Every campaign runs on people willing to do the unglamorous work of keeping information organized. That's a real, transferable skill, and it lasts well past the summer.
