Cover Letter Templates That Actually Work for Civic Roles

By YourTurn2Intern Staff

Writing a cover letter can feel like the hardest part of applying, especially when you don't have a long resume to lean on. The good news: civic, government, and campaign internships usually care more about genuine interest and reliability than years of experience. A good cover letter just needs to show you understand the organization and you're serious about contributing.

The basic structure

  • An opening hook: one or two sentences that show real interest, not a generic "I am writing to apply for..."
  • Why this organization specifically: mention something concrete about their work, not just "I admire your mission."
  • What you bring: even with limited experience, you have something: a class project, volunteer work, organizing skills, comfort with people, attention to detail.
  • A clear closing: restate your interest and invite them to reach out.

Template: Government office internship

I'm writing to apply for the internship position with [Organization Name]. I first became interested in local government after [specific experience: attending a town board meeting, a class project, a personal connection to an issue], and I've followed [Organization Name]'s work on [Specific Issue/Program] since then. While I haven't worked in an office setting before, I've developed strong research and communication skills through [school project, volunteer work, club leadership], and I'm comfortable learning new systems quickly. I'd welcome the chance to support your team and learn directly from the people doing this work. Thank you for considering my application. I'd love to talk further about how I can contribute.

Template: Campaign internship

I'm reaching out to apply for an internship with [Candidate/Campaign Name]. [Specific Issue/Program] is something I care about, and I want to put in the work to help elect someone who will act on it. I may be new to campaigns, but I'm organized, comfortable talking to people I don't know, and ready to do whatever the team needs: phone calls, canvassing, data entry, event support. I show up on time and I follow through. I'd be grateful for the opportunity to contribute to this campaign and learn from your team along the way.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Being too generic. If your letter could apply to any organization, it won't stand out. Name something specific about this office, candidate, or cause.
  • Apologizing for lack of experience. Don't open with "I don't have much experience, but..." Instead, lead with what you do bring.
  • Forgetting to proofread. A typo in the organization's name is an easy way to seem careless.
  • Writing it once and reusing it everywhere. Always swap in real specifics. Readers can tell when a letter was copy-pasted.

Treat your cover letter as a short, honest pitch. Say who you are, why you care about this work, and what you'll bring if they give you the chance. That's all it needs to do.
