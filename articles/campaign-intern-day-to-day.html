What Does a Campaign Intern Actually Do?

By Jeremy Fang

Campaign internships sound exciting in theory, and they are, but the day-to-day work is a lot less glamorous than what you see in movies or on TV. There's no war room with dramatic music playing in the background. Mostly, it's a folding table, a clipboard, and a list of phone numbers. That doesn't mean it isn't valuable. It just means it helps to know what you're actually signing up for.

The Core Tasks

Most campaign interns spend their time on a rotating set of jobs:

  • Canvassing: Walking neighborhoods, knocking on doors, and talking with voters about the candidate or issue. This is often the single most common task, and it builds confidence in talking to strangers fast.
  • Phone banking: Calling voters from a list, reading a script, and logging their responses. It's repetitive, but it's also one of the most direct ways campaigns reach people.
  • Data entry: After canvassing or phone banking, someone has to enter all that information into a database: who supports the candidate, who's undecided, who asked for a yard sign. That someone is often an intern.
  • Event setup: Setting up chairs, signage, and sound equipment before a rally or town hall, then breaking it all down afterward.
  • Social media support: Drafting posts, taking photos at events, or helping monitor comments, usually under close supervision from a communications staffer.

Set Your Expectations Accordingly

None of this is particularly flashy. You probably won't be in the room when big strategy decisions get made, and you might spend an entire afternoon doing nothing but data entry. But these tasks matter more than they seem. Campaigns run on direct contact with voters, and every door knocked on or call made is a tiny piece of a much bigger effort. Interns who do the unglamorous work well are the ones who get trusted with more responsibility as the campaign goes on.

The Hours Can Get Long, Especially Near Election Day

Campaign work doesn't run on a typical 9-to-5 schedule. Evenings and weekends are often the best times to reach voters at home, so expect some shifts outside normal school hours. As Election Day approaches, the pace picks up significantly. This is often called the "get out the vote" push, and it can mean long days, early mornings, and a lot of last-minute scrambling. If you're juggling school or another job, talk to the campaign staff early about your availability so everyone's expectations line up.

Despite the long hours and repetitive tasks, most campaign interns walk away with something hard to get anywhere else: real comfort talking to strangers, a behind-the-scenes understanding of how elections actually work, and a front-row seat to a process that shapes who represents your community.
